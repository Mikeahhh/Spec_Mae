Sound-UAV: Energy-Efficient Search and Rescue via Biomimetic Sentinel-Responder Sensing

Affiliation purposefully removed for double-blind review

Abstract—Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) search and rescue (SAR) missions are often constrained by obscured and lowvisibility conditions; thus, acoustic sensing technology is considered a highly promising non-contact SAR method. Traditional acoustic UAV systems are limited by onboard battery life, where existing ”always-on” solutions consume excessive energy, while simple duty-cycling modes may miss transient distress signals due to wake-up latency. To address this issue, we propose SoundUAV, a sentinel-responder perception system featuring a duallayer architecture. Crucially, these two stages are bridged by an asymmetric Ring Buffer, which retains continuous audio data and supports retrospective signal processing, thereby ensuring zero information loss for transient signals. Meanwhile, in the sentinel stage, we introduce a low-power ”sentinel” module driven by a multi-scenario pre-training strategy. This module discards fixed masking rate limitations and learns a robust background noise manifold by modeling a parameter space encompassing variable wind noise and rotor states, triggering the multi-channel ”responder” only when spectral anomalies are detected. Simulation results demonstrate that Sound-UAV can effectively optimize masking rates across different scenarios, maintaining robust geometric localization accuracy while significantly reducing idle power consumption.
Index Terms—UAV, Search and Rescue, Acoustic Sensing, Anomaly Detection, TDOA
I. INTRODUCTION
The growing popularity of outdoor recreational activities has exacerbated the need for rapid search and rescue (SAR) operations in complex terrains. In these rescue missions, professional rescuers are required, and the ability to quickly localize trapped individuals is the key to outdoor SAR tasks. Compared to existing UAV rescue technologies, each modality exhibits significant limitations. Visual systems, while highly advanced in object detection and tracking, are inherently constrained by line-of-sight (LOS) obstructions such as dense forest canopies or thick fog. Similarly, thermal infrared (TIR) imaging is effective for night operations but lacks robustness in high-heat background environments or when a survivor’s thermal signature is obscured by debris or thick clothing. In contrast, acoustic sensing and localization technology is viewed as a highly potential non-contact SAR method due to its ability to penetrate visual obstacles (like heavy smoke and vegetation) and respond to the active cries for help from trapped victims. As a passive and omnidirectional sensing modality, audio can provide reliable spatio-temporal information even when visual or radio frequency signals are severely distorted. Classical research on microphone array processing and Time Difference of Arrival (TDOA) estimation has laid the theoretical foundation for sound source localization and has been widely applied in robot audition, surveillance, and human-computer interaction systems.
However, traditional acoustic localization relies on ”alwayson” multi-channel microphone arrays running complex beamforming algorithms, which introduces massive computational overhead and energy consumption. Maintaining continuous multi-channel acquisition and running compute-intensive algorithms, such as Generalized Cross-Correlation (GCC-PHAT) or Multiple Signal Classification (MUSIC), imposes a tremendous burden on the limited battery life of UAVs, significantly restricting the operational radius of rescue missions. Crucially, these studies overlook the temporal asymmetry of SAR missions; during flights lasting several hours, the blind search phase (lacking effective target signals) often occupies more than 90% of the total mission time. Maintaining a fullpower microphone array and high-frequency spatial filtering during these prolonged silent periods is computationally redundant, directly violating the energy-efficient principles of edge intelligence. Conversely, while low-power periodic sleep architectures have been widely explored, they often result in an intolerable miss-detection rate for transient signals.
To overcome these challenges, we propose Sound-UAV, an energy-efficient dual-layer perception system. This system strictly decouples the task into a single-channel, low-power ”sentinel” for continuous environmental monitoring, and an on-demand, high-precision multi-channel ”responder”.
The main contributions of this paper can be summarized in three dimensions:
•     Sentinel-Responder Architecture: We propose a duallayer perception framework that completely decouples anomaly detection from spatial localization. To eliminate hardware wake-up latency, these two systems are seamlessly connected by a DMA-based ring buffer, which retains audio data monitored in sentinel mode and supports retrospective signal processing, ensuring zero information loss for transient signals emitted by victims.
•     Multi-Scenario Adaptive SpecMAE Model: Inspired by multi-modal autoencoder mechanisms, we innovatively design a lightweight Spectrogram Masked Autoencoder (SpecMAE) on the sentinel module. To overcome the extreme diversity of UAV physical environments, we discard the limitations of fixed masking rates and single scenarios, introducing a multi-scenario pre-training strategy. By constructing a parameter space manifold covering environmental variables, rotor states, and wind noise, the model learns a robust latent representation of the ”normal” onboard acoustic environment, enabling it to accurately strip background noise and trigger anomalies in any unknown environment.
•     Comprehensive Benchmark Experiments: We conducted extensive simulation experiments to validate the system. The results demonstrate that this architecture possesses overwhelming advantages in energy efficiency, response performance, and localization accuracy in highly challenging multipath and high-noise SAR environments.
II. SYSTEM MODEL
In this section, we introduce the UAV outdoor search and rescue process based on the dual-layer architecture of SoundUAV. First, we review the fundamental principles of the Sound-UAV dual-layer structure, describe how the SpecMAE model in sentinel mode accomplishes noise detection, and explain the ring buffer that ensures no loss of audio signals after triggering the localization mode. Based on this, we address the personnel localization problem for UAVs in various outdoor scenarios.

A. Hardware Framework
On our UAV, we consider a UAV microphone array represented by the set M = {0,1,...,M − 1}. We partition this set into two functional subsets: the Sentinel Node (m0), an omnidirectional microphone located at the geometric center of the body coordinate system, denoted as b0 = [0,0,0]T. It dedicatedly runs the SpecMAE model to perform continuous background filtering and identify abnormal signals. The second is the Responder Set Maux = {1,...,M − 1}, a Uniform Circular Array (UCA) consisting of K − 1 = 8 microphones equally distributed on a planar circle of radius r. This is used for high-precision localization when triggered.

1)   Sentinel Mode (Sk = 0): In this default state, the system minimizes its energy footprint. The sentinel node (Msen) utilizes the single-node microphone (m0) to continuously sample the acoustic environment and runs the SpecMAE model, monitoring spectral anomalies in real-time by comparing features with the pre-learned steady-state environmental noise manifold.
2)   Data Bridge: During state 0, the responder array consisting of 8 microphones continuously pushes multi-channel raw audio frames cyclically into an isolated SRAM ring buffer via a Direct Memory Access (DMA) controller. This lowlevel hardware interaction entirely bypasses the sleeping CPU, ensuring that the physical envelope of sudden acoustic events is losslessly captured with zero computational power overhead.
3)   Responder Mode (Sk = 1): Upon detecting an anomaly, the system wakes up the high-performance core. The system retrieves historical data from the ring buffer (covering the duration of the detected anomaly) and executes GCC-PHAT spatial localization, utilizing TDOA and DOA estimation to calculate the victim’s position. The Sentinel remains active during this phase. It continuously monitors the signal stream to detect when the anomaly (e.g., screaming) ceases. This generates a real-time termination signal, prompting the system to return to the sentinel mode, thereby preventing the highpower mode from running longer than necessary.

III. SPECMAE AND PRE-TRAINING
This paper proposes an acoustically optimized Vision Transformer (ViT) pipeline, a lightweight Spectrogram Masked Autoencoder (SpecMAE) optimized for acoustics. Unlike multimodal architectures, SpecMAE focuses on modeling the steady-state background noise manifold of single-channel audio. The model consists of four main components: input embedding, encoder, latent feature space, and decoder.

A. Acoustic Feature Representation
The sentinel module processes the single-channel audio stream ymono(t). The input audio stream is segmented into 1second observation frames of length Ls and mapped to a logMel spectrogram X ∈ RF×T to capture the time-frequency energy distribution. To adapt to the Transformer architecture, the spectrogram is uniformly divided into non-overlapping patches of size P × P (e.g., 16 × 16). These patches are flattened and linearly projected to become embedding vectors. The total number of tokens fed into the model is proportional to the frame length:

1)   Encoder: The encoder fenc consists of a series of Transformer blocks. During the pre-training and inference phases, we mask the input patches using a scenario-specific random masking rate (e.g., ρ = 75%) based on the different environments being searched. The encoder only processes the retained visible patches, thereby significantly reducing the computational load on the DSP:

2)   Latent Space Projection: To achieve a smooth transition of features and extract deep manifold structures, we project the encoded feature representations into a latent feature space. We use a linear projection layer followed by Layer Normalization (LN):
                                     Z = LN(WH + b)                                         (7)
where W is the projection matrix, H is the feature representation output by the encoder, and b is the corresponding bias term. The latent representation Z compactly encapsulates the core acoustic structure of steady-state noise (such as rotor harmonics and wind noise).
3)   Decoder: The goal of the decoder fdec is to reconstruct the masked spectrogram patches from the latent features. It takes the latent representation Z combined with a set of learnable mask tokens as input and processes them through a series of lightweight Transformer blocks. Subsequently, the output is mapped back to the original patch pixel space via a projection layer:
                                     Xˆ = fproj(fdec(Z))                                        (8)
where fproj is a linear projection layer of specific dimensions, and Xˆ is the complete log-Mel spectrogram that the model attempts to reconstruct.

2) Online Inference and Adaptive Trigger: In online search and rescue missions, when high-rank transient signals (nonstationary patterns) such as human cries intrude, the model cannot utilize steady-state context for reconstruction, causing the aforementioned Lrec to exhibit a step-like mutation. The system applies a 300-millisecond sliding window to this loss to extract a smoothed expectation µrec(t). When µrec(t) exceeds an adaptive threshold γ dynamically updated with the environmental noise floor, the system outputs a hardware interrupt instruction D = 1, thereby waking up the main NPU to enter State 1.

A. Space-MAE Context-Aware Anomaly Detection
We first evaluated the sensitivity of the Space-MAE Sentinel running on the low-power DSP. As illustrated in Fig. 3 (Fig A), during the initial phase (0.0 to 1.0s), the reconstruction loss (Lrec) remains highly stable and close to the baseline (approximately 0.0004). This indicates that the pre-trained Space-MAE has successfully modeled the continuous, predictable manifold of the drone’s ego-noise, effectively avoiding false alarms.
At t = 1.0s, a simulated transient human distress signal is introduced. Because this high-rank signal fundamentally breaks the learned structural flow of the drone noise manifold, the model fails to reconstruct it, causing an immediate and sharp spike in Lrec (peaking near 0.0035). This instantaneous surge flawlessly breaches the dynamically assigned adaptive threshold (γth = 0.001), seamlessly triggering the State 1 hardware interrupt to awaken the Responder array.
B. Retroactive Ring Buffer and Localization Integrity
To demonstrate the absolute necessity of the DMA-backed ring buffer, we compared Sound-UAV against a standard dutycycled system lacking retroactive capabilities. The test was conducted in a dense forest scenario featuring intense non-lineof-sight (NLOS) multipath reflections and a high reverberation time (RT60 = 0.6s).

As shown in Fig. 3 (Fig B), a traditional wake-up system misses the critical first hundreds of milliseconds of the transient signal due to hardware latency. Consequently, the GCCPHAT algorithm is forced to process the multipath tails and echoes, resulting in a severe Direction of Arrival (DOA) error of 38.5◦, far exceeding the 5◦ target convergence tolerance. Conversely, Sound-UAV retrieves the historical raw audio from the SRAM buffer. By capturing the direct Line-of-Sight (LoS) signal onset with zero information loss, our system elegantly suppresses multipath interference, achieving a highly precise DOA error of just 2.1◦.
C. System Energy Efficiency and Flight Endurance
Finally, we quantified the macro-level engineering impact of our dual-stage architecture. Fig. 4 illustrates the stark contrast between a continuous ”Always-On” multi-microphone array and the Sound-UAV framework over a standardized 30-minute SAR mission.
By offloading continuous monitoring to the micro-power CMAD and maintaining the NPU in deep sleep, Sound-UAV reduces the total acoustic computing energy from nearly 27 kJ to a negligible amount, achieving a 97.9% reduction (Fig C). More importantly for real-world SAR operations, this massive decrease in computational drain translates directly into extended flight capacity. As shown in Fig D, deploying SoundUAV extends the micro-UAV’s effective hovering endurance from 36 minutes to 51 minutes, securing an additional 15.0 minutes of critical search time.
V. CONCLUSION
We proposed Sound-UAV, an energy-efficient acoustic perception system for UAV SAR missions. The framework centers on a dual-stage Sentinel-Responder architecture bridged by a retroactive ring buffer. By utilizing a scenario-adaptive SpecMAE design to maintain low idle power and leveraging historical data to eliminate wake-up information loss, the system achieves significant energy savings while maintaining robust spatial localization.
